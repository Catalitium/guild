Mathematics

If anything holds the crumbling edges of this universe together, it's probably math. Not your high school worksheets, but the raw machinery humming behind orbits, spirals, markets, memory. It's old—older than logic—and possibly incomplete, and yet we keep scratching at it like apes tapping on an alien radio, convinced we can tune into something more than just ourselves.

Core Concepts

Mathematics doesn’t answer as much as it frames the asking. It builds languages to trap truth, or at least give it a shape. Geometry gave us the gods of the Greeks, calculus split motion into infinitesimals, logic teased apart paradoxes—and Gödel laughed. This section outlines the key conceptual scaffolds, not as subjects, but as the whispering organs of an unfinished system.

Arithmetic

The study of numbers and operations—ancient and foundational, used in everything from trade to cryptography.

Geometry

The shape of the world, the tension of angles, the postulates of Euclid. Still relevant, still expanding.

Algebra

From Persian al-jabr: solving for the unknown. It lets us speak symbolically, abstractly, infinitely.

Calculus

Invented in parallel by Newton and Leibniz. Measures change, motion, and growth. A blade for time itself.

Set Theory

The skeleton of modern mathematics. Cantor’s naive infinities and Zermelo-Fraenkel’s formal axioms underlie it all.

Logic

From Aristotle to Russell, logic refines reasoning. It’s the toolkit for proving what is—or isn’t—provable.

Mathematical Dictionary

  • Proof: A logical argument establishing the truth of a theorem.
  • Theorem: A statement that has been proven based on axioms and logic.
  • Function: A relation between inputs and outputs with defined rules.
  • Limit: The value a function approaches as input approaches some value.
  • Infinity: A concept, not a number. Bigger than anything you can count.
  • Group: A set with a binary operation satisfying closure, identity, inverses, and associativity.
  • Field: A number system where you can add, subtract, multiply, and divide (except by zero).
  • Vector: An object with magnitude and direction, used in physics and linear algebra.
  • Matrix: A rectangular array of numbers—like a spreadsheet with muscle.
  • Derivative: Instantaneous rate of change—calculus' razor.
  • Integral: Area under a curve. Anti-derivative. Accumulator.
  • Algorithm: A step-by-step procedure to solve a problem, whether numerical or logical.
  • Combinatorics: The art of counting cleverly.
  • Topology: The mathematics of continuity, holes, and the shapes of things without shapes.
  • Number Theory: The playground of primes, divisibility, and ancient questions.

Foundational Texts & Theorems

  • Elements (Euclid, ~300 BCE) — The geometry blueprint. Definitions, postulates, and theorems still in classrooms today.
  • Principia Mathematica (Whitehead & Russell, 1910) — An attempt to derive all math from logic. Three volumes. Took 362 pages to prove 1+1=2.
  • On Formally Undecidable Propositions (Kurt Gödel, 1931) — Gödel's incompleteness theorems shattered certainty: some truths can't be proven.
  • Hilbert's Problems (David Hilbert, 1900) — 23 problems presented as math’s frontier for the 20th century. Some solved, some still haunting.
  • Disquisitiones Arithmeticae (Carl Friedrich Gauss, 1801) — The launch pad of modern number theory.
  • Fermat's Last Theorem (solved by Andrew Wiles, 1994) — 350 years unsolved. Then proven with elliptic curves and modular forms.
  • The Method (Archimedes, ~250 BCE) — Precursor to calculus. Balanced geometric intuition with rigorous logic.
